Teaching

Teaching philosophy and experience.

Philosophy

Modern AI looks magical from a distance and merely linear-algebraic up close. The gap between those two views is where most students get stuck — and where most of the interesting security questions live. My teaching aims to bridge it: starting from the linear algebra, probability, and optimization that the field is actually built on, and walking forward until students can read a recent attack paper and tell, themselves, why it works.

In an AI security course in particular, abstraction is not a luxury — it is the subject. A student who internalizes the geometry of the embedding space sees adversarial examples, template inversion, and certified robustness as variations on a single theme. A student who only memorizes pipelines does not.

Concretely, I lean on three habits:

  • Anchor every result to its assumption. An attack that assumes white-box access is a different object from the same attack under black-box access; conflating them is the most common mistake in the literature, and the easiest to teach away.
  • Reproduce before generalizing. Students implement at least one attack and one defense from scratch before reading survey papers. This catches the silent assumptions that only show up in code.
  • Read like an adversary. Every defense reading is paired with an exercise: where would you attack this? The goal is to build the reflex of asking what the security argument actually proves — and what it quietly assumes.
