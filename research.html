Research

My work pursues a single thesis: that the security of modern AI systems is governed, at the deepest level, by the geometry of their representation space. Most state-of-the-art models — face recognizers, speaker identifiers, vision–language models — embed their inputs into a high-dimensional hypersphere; the way that hypersphere is partitioned determines what an attacker can extract and what a defender can hide. My research treats this geometry as the primary object of study, rather than as background, and uses it to organize three research threads.

01 · Attack

Hyperspherical geometry and non-adaptive attacks

Modern face-recognition pipelines map an input to an embedding on a high-dimensional hypersphere $\mathbb{S}^{d-1}$. Within that hypersphere sit identity subspaces — regions in which embeddings of the same person concentrate. Once that geometry is understood, surprisingly little information is needed to invert a template or impersonate an identity.

Most prior black-box attacks on face recognition rely on adaptive query strategies: tens of thousands of queries, each one chosen in response to the last. My work introduced the first non-adaptive counterpart — attacks that fix all queries up front. Using only a handful of images and a single batch query, the attack reconstructs a target identity or generates a perturbation-free impersonation image, and it succeeds against deployed commercial APIs (Amazon Rekognition, Tencent, among others). This line of work appeared at IEEE S&P 2024 and NeurIPS 2025.

Where it goes next

The non-adaptive framework extends naturally beyond faces. Palmprint recognition, voice biometrics, and the identity-verification stacks increasingly used in finance and authentication all live on similar hyperspherical embedding spaces, and all inherit the same structural weaknesses. A current direction analyses how the source–target embedding distance controls the difficulty of a targeted attack, and frames a query-budget splitting strategy as an optimization problem — work targeted at IEEE TPAMI.

02 · Defense

Privacy-preserving defenses for noisy embeddings

Passwords match exactly. Biometrics, prompts, and the visual inputs of a vision-language model do not — every measurement carries a different noise realization, and the resulting embeddings drift. This non-determinism breaks the standard cryptographic playbook: encrypting the template directly destroys matchability; brute-force homomorphic comparison is too slow to deploy.

Biometric template protection (BTP) wraps embeddings in transformations that remain matchable but, ideally, irreversible. The catch is that many published schemes — locality-sensitive hashing, random projection, learned transformations — turn out to be reversible under modern decoders. My work has produced both schemes and analyses: IronMask (CVPR 2021) and Deep Face Template Protection in the Wild (Pattern Recognition 2025) combine real-valued error-correcting codes with hash functions to protect templates without sacrificing accuracy; IDFace (ICCV 2025) brings the same idea to large-scale identification rather than verification; and SilverMask (IEEE Access 2026) introduces a fine-grained noise-correction mechanism. On the analysis side, IEEE TDSC 2025 gives a unified account of when LSH-based protections are reversible — and when they are not.

Where it goes next

The natural next step is to combine non-cryptographic structures (LSH, learned indices) with cryptographic primitives (FHE, MPC) under a single security analysis. A recent direction connects this to fuzzy private set intersection for high-dimensional cosine similarity — bringing the privacy-preserving retrieval problem into the same geometric framework as the attack work above.

03 · Certify

Certified robustness for recognition systems

Empirical robustness is fragile — a recognizer that resists today's attacks can fall to tomorrow's. Certified robustness sidesteps the arms race by giving a mathematical guarantee: no adversarial perturbation within a specified radius can flip the decision. Adapting this framework to recognition is non-trivial: the standard randomized-smoothing toolbox assumes classification, while recognition lives on a metric space.

Our work at ECCV 2024 and IEEE T-BIOM 2025 studies how certified-robustness analyses transfer to recognition geometry, identifies where the assumptions break, and proposes targeted fixes.

04 · Foundation models

The same geometry, at scale

The hyperspherical embedding spaces studied above are not specific to biometrics. CLIP — the visual backbone behind most modern vision-language models — uses the same geometric primitives, and inherits many of the same vulnerabilities. A current line of work uses high-level prompting to iteratively refine images so as to drift the CLIP decision boundary, defeating modern deepfake detectors without touching pixel-level perturbations.

The flip side is that strong attacks produce the highest-purity adversarial training data we have. The endgame for this thread is a closed loop in the spirit of GANs: use mathematically grounded attacks to generate the worst-case data, then use that data to train recognizers and detectors that are demonstrably more robust.

Connecting tissue

The four threads share a substrate: the geometry of the embedding space. Once that geometry is taken seriously, attack and defense stop looking like separate problems — they become two sides of the same optimization, with certified robustness as the third constraint, and foundation models as the setting where all three suddenly matter at once.
