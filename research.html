Research

The work sits at the intersection of applied mathematics, biometric security, and computer vision. Three threads run through it.

01 · Attack

What scores leak about identity

Modern face recognition systems return a similarity score. Treated naively, the score looks like a single number — a thin channel. But under the right geometric assumptions, repeated queries to a black-box recognizer reveal enough about the underlying embedding space to reconstruct a target face, even without adaptive feedback.

My work in this direction shows that non-adaptive attacks — attacks that fix all queries up front — can be both query-efficient and surprisingly effective. The published work appeared at IEEE S&P 2024 (Scores Tell Everything about Bob) and NeurIPS 2025 (Non-Adaptive Adversarial Face Generation). A current direction is a budget-splitting strategy framed as an optimization problem over source–target embedding distance, targeting an extension toward IEEE TPAMI.

02 · Defense

Template protection that survives

Once an attacker can read the embedding, the natural response is to never store it in the clear. Biometric template protection (BTP) wraps embeddings in transformations that are matchable but, ideally, irreversible. The catch: many published schemes — locality-sensitive hashing, random projections, learned transformations — are reversible under modern decoders.

I work on schemes with explicit security analyses, including IDFace (ICCV 2025), which targets identification rather than verification, and SilverMask (IEEE Access 2026), which uses fine-grained noise correction to survive against adaptive recovery. A companion line of work (IEEE TDSC 2025) gives a unified analysis of when LSH-based protections are reversible — and when they are not.

03 · Certify

Robustness with proofs

Empirical robustness is fragile. A recognizer that resists today's attacks can fall to tomorrow's. Certified robustness sidesteps the arms race by giving a mathematical guarantee: no adversarial perturbation within a specified radius can flip the decision. Bringing this to face recognition is non-trivial — the standard randomized smoothing toolbox assumes classification, while recognition lives on a metric space.

Our recent work (IEEE T-BIOM 2025, Towards Certifiably Robust Face Recognition) studies how the certified-robustness framework adapts to recognition geometry, and where its assumptions break.

Connecting tissue

The three threads share a common substrate: the geometry of the embedding space. Once that geometry is taken seriously, attack and defense stop looking like separate problems — they become two sides of the same optimization, with certification as the third constraint.
